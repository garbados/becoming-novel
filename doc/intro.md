# What is Utopia? Or: a preface to the novel *Becoming*

## After ["Is Gender Necessary? Redux" by Ursula K Le Guin](https://theanarchistlibrary.org/library/ursula-k-le-guin-is-gender-necessary-redux)

Someday I will revise this letter, by way of annotations rather than edits. Today is not that day.

...

Let me ask you an annoying question: what is the perfect society?

You might ask, like I would, "What do you mean by perfect?"

I can only shrug and reply, "What does it mean to you?"

...

I wanted to give a trans person a childhood, like was denied and prevented for me and so many others. Our post-industrial practices, like HRT, are profound iterations on millennia of documented gender fuckery around the world. A term like "third-gender" only scratches the surface of the universes humans have created and endured, trying to answer a sacred mystery the only way they know how: by living their reply. The illusion of dimorphism tempts us; what can one ever say is truly not an illusion, in a world -- an experience, governed by the mind?

Trans people, regardless of their relationship to a gender binary, exist in the vacuum of a multi-millennial occlusion spread by the death drives of colony and capital. It has never made sense to describe their violence against us as a *genocide*, because we are not a *gen*, we are neither a *genetic* nor *geographic* legacy. We are undeniable. Unlike any landed or blooded peoples, we cannot be killed. The worst that the mightiest forces of brutality and domination have ever been able to accomplish even at the heights of their own supremacy, has been to render us unthinkable. No earthly power is immortal; even death may die, and the kyriarchy lacks the reaper's stamina. They hold back a dam with a thumb that is already rotting away.

As a writer -- in that it is a habit I cannot kick, which is the most spiritually fulfilling form of expression to-date experienced, which has paid a sum total of $90 in my lifetime -- I observe how the place-times before and after or outside of this particular modernity become real places, with living people in them. *Everything for Everyone* depicts a sort of in-progress militant kindness, the tenuous unity of the survivors of revolutions around the world *barely* and *luckily* finding common ground in the wake of empire, to lick the wounds accrued over countless generations with cruelty, today one's inevitable companion.

In my faith, it is said: *if Utopia is love, then it begins by loving yourself.* This is meant both as a commitment -- that one *should* love themselves -- and a question of practice: if one does not know *how* to love even their own self, then how can they know how to love anyone else? You are your own closest reference for the Creator that reflects in all of us. Like the illusion of fantasies of *after **the** revolution*, imagining that Utopia is a singular place -- that there might exist a perfect government, much less such a society -- crashes upon the chaos of being, dying, being reborn, and facing different choices differently each time. Empires crumble; even languages go extinct. So the question becomes a sacred mystery: "how does one love?" cannot be answered, but only attempted.

To give a trans person a childhood, which contains something like sacred forms of so-called *real love*, one either chooses to confront how such childhoods are prevented, or to write those causes out of the universe. I chose not to write fantasy or science fiction, but a coming-of-age romance among high school seniors. That was my experiment in Utopia. I conducted it while my marriage approached divorce, tens of thousands of words dedicated to a question that I slowly found I could not answer. I wrote about Billie and Skyler falling in love until I moved out and served papers.

Then I tried again. Utopia calls.

...

Pain-eaters will pay handsomely for a serving of your suffering. Ryan Coogler knows it. Only the wise and just-clever-enough may maneuver the momentum and inertia of such a devil's bargain. The pain-eater seeks, and the author -- being naturally pleased by readership -- grants it. We want to get the word out, yes? ***Give me your words***, *mutters the pain-eater, coins spilling from clawed hands.* If that spirit cannot yet be buried, then I suppose we must live alongside it. Speak, and let it hear, coins be damned. Fleece the rich, while the hustle's good, but it is your truth that you shepherd in a life, not another's purse. As the money is always bloody, however crusted-over by the time it reaches you, one can only pay or be paid in tokens empowered our enemy's machinations. They are never, ever the point. They are the stuff of a small wedge in the broad front toward Utopia. Let the pain-eaters hear me say *faggot* umpteen times in the first chapter, and half of those instances mean, "I love you."

My suffering is beautiful, but that beauty is not for you. It is not about you. I look good in the mirror, I look good when I feel bad, I feel good when I look bad, and when my panties say, "You eat here because we let you," I mean it. I grace you with the radiance of my flawed facets of love, as you do. That is not about some intimate partner: that is how I feel about the word of the poet, the speaker, the storyteller. That is what moves me when Billie faces not that she must *begin* HRT, but what it means that she *did*; and when she asks, what is left by all I did to do so? She suffers because of real things, however placeless the nameless town is, the nameless state, the nameless capitol. Fiction cannot help but tell the truth; a truth cannot help but reveal.

But the point is not that she suffers, pain-eater. The point is she experiences love, inside and out. That is Utopia. It cannot be killed or destroyed, only prevented. This time, for however brief a moment, it wasn't.
